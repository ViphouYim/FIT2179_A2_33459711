Why heat-health risk matters

National burden

Time pattern

Heat injury is a repeated health burden, not a single-year spike.

Proportion

Heat accounts for nearly four in five recorded extreme-weather injury hospitalisations

78% of all recorded extreme-weather injury hospitalisations were heat-related. 7,104 of 9,119 cases were linked to extreme heat.

Heat is the largest weather-health burden nationally. The next question is whether this risk is evenly distributed across Australia — and who carries the greatest share.

How to read

Where to look

  • Northern Australia — high exposure + vulnerability
  • Remote regions — stronger compound risk
  • Major cities — generally lower but locally varied
Excluded regions
  • Other Territories (4 SA2s): Christmas Island, Cocos (Keeling) Islands, Jervis Bay, Norfolk Island
  • No resident population (~142 SA2s): national parks, industrial zones, airports, ports and military areas
  • Migratory / offshore / shipping zones: no fixed residents

What drives heat-health risk?

Compound pressure

Individual drivers explain local scores. The next question is whether some states are systematically more exposed than others — and how much variation exists within each state.

04 —

Risk levels differ sharply between states

State summaries reveal broad patterns, but large within-state variation means individual SA2 data always matters.

What drives each state's risk?

NT and WA risk profiles are driven more strongly by exposure

Normalised bars show relative component contribution, not total HHRI.

Takeaway: The Northern Territory records the highest relative exposure component of any state or territory. NSW and Victoria show more balanced mixes — targeted responses will need to differ by region.

Policy implication

NT & WA needInfrastructure and climate-adaptation responses — the dominant exposure bar means built-environment solutions come first
NSW & VIC needIntegrated multi-strand programmes — no single component dominates, so housing, health and vulnerability must all be addressed

How much do areas vary within each state?

State averages hide large local differences

Box plots show HHRI scores across SA2s within each state. A wide box means SA2s within that state differ strongly from each other.

Takeaway: State-level averages can hide sharp local differences. This is why SA2-level mapping remains essential for identifying where heat-health risk is concentrated.

Spread at a glance

NTnarrowest spread — SA2s are uniformly high-risk with little local variation
NSWwidest spread — urban areas lower the median despite significant high-risk pockets

NT and WA show the highest relative exposure component, meaning environmental heat pressure is the primary driver in those regions. NSW and Victoria show more balanced profiles — different states need different responses. Within each state, wide HHRI variation confirms that SA2-level data always matters more than state averages.
