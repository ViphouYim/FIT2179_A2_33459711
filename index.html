Why heat-health risk matters

National burden

Heat causes the largest injury burden

Takeaway: Heat is the clearest starting point for understanding weather-related health risk in Australia.

Time pattern

Peak years are labelled to highlight the main trend quickly.

Hospitalisations fluctuate across the decade, with several years passing 1,000 recorded cases.

Takeaway: Extreme weather injury is not a one-off problem; it is a recurring pressure on health systems.

Proportion

Heat dominates the burden

Most recorded extreme weather injury hospitalisations were linked to heat.

Takeaway: This explains why the rest of the report focuses on heat-health risk.

02

Where the risk is concentrated

Heat-health risk is uneven across Australia. The map reveals where exposure, vulnerability and adaptive capacity combine into higher local risk.

Spatial pattern

Heat-health risk is not evenly spread

Hover each SA2 to inspect local risk conditions and compare overall HHRI scores across regions.

The SA2 map shows the overall Heat-Health Risk Index, where darker areas have higher relative risk.

Takeaway: The map should not be read as temperature alone. It combines heat exposure with social, housing, health and adaptive-capacity factors.

How to read this map

Darker SA2s have higher relative heat-health risk. The score combines heat exposure, social and housing vulnerability, health status and adaptive capacity, so it should not be read as temperature alone.

  • Exposure: local heat and environmental pressure
  • Vulnerability: housing, health and social disadvantage
  • Adaptive capacity: support access and local resilience

Use this as a guide: first scan for high-risk clusters, then hover to identify which component is driving risk in each SA2.

Hotspot ranking

Ranking the top SA2s helps identify the places with the highest combined heat-health risk.

Takeaway: Heat-health risk is not limited to major cities; regional and remote areas also appear among the highest-risk places.

State comparison

Risk levels make the map easier to read

Quintiles divide SA2s into five comparable risk groups from lowest to highest.

What drives risk?

Risk is highest where multiple pressures overlap. These charts separate the index into exposure, housing, health and social vulnerability.

Driver overlap

High exposure becomes more serious when vulnerability is also high

This chart compares heat exposure with social vulnerability across SA2s.

Takeaway: The most concerning regions sit where both exposure and vulnerability are high.

Dual pressure

Housing and health risks can overlap

Housing condition risk and health status risk are compared directly.

This chart compares housing condition risk with health status risk.

Takeaway: Heat-health risk becomes more serious when poorer housing conditions coincide with poorer health status.

Population structure

Older communities add another layer of risk

Bubble size represents population.

Older residents are more vulnerable to heat stress, especially in areas with high exposure.

Takeaway: Age vulnerability is spatially uneven, so local population structure matters.

Social pattern

Disadvantage helps explain uneven risk

Lower SEIFA scores indicate greater disadvantage.

Lower SEIFA IRSD scores indicate greater socioeconomic disadvantage.

Takeaway: Heat-health risk is shaped by social conditions, not only by climate conditions.

Final focus: the highest-risk areas

Final synthesis

The highest-risk SA2s share multiple risk drivers

This heatmap shows which risk components contribute most strongly in the top-ranked areas.

Takeaway: Priority areas are not high-risk for one reason alone. Most combine high exposure with vulnerability, housing or health pressures.

How to read this chart

Darker cells indicate higher component scores. Compare each row to see how risk composition changes across the top 10 SA2s.

Most high-risk areas do not score high on every component. Instead, each region shows a different mix of exposure, housing, health, vulnerability, and adaptive capacity.

Why this matters: targeted local responses work better than a single nationwide solution.

What this means

Heat-health risk is not simply a map of hot places. The highest-risk SA2s are places where heat exposure combines with social vulnerability, housing condition risk, health status risk and limited adaptive capacity. Mapping these factors together makes the risk easier to see, compare and act on.

Domain, Audience, and Method

Domain: climate-related heat-health risk in Australian SA2 regions.

Who: public health planners, local councils, emergency managers, and policy audiences.

What: a multi-chart dashboard integrating national burden, spatial risk, drivers, and top-risk profiles.

Why: to identify where heat-health risk is highest and which factors should be targeted first.

How: ACS/ABS HHRI data joined to SA2 geography, analysed with comparative and multivariate Vega-Lite views.
