01 —

Why heat-health risk matters

National injury data shows why extreme heat is the starting point before mapping where heat-health risk is highest.

Key finding

Heat is the dominant weather-health burden

Extreme heat records 7,104 injury hospitalisations — far above bushfire, cold, rain and storms combined.

Several years exceed 1,000 cases, confirming this is a recurring and serious health burden.

National burden

Extreme heat causes more injury hospitalisations than all other listed hazards combined.

Time pattern

Heat injury is a repeated health burden, not a single-year spike.

Proportion

Heat dominates the burden

78% of all recorded extreme-weather injury hospitalisations were heat-related. 7,104 of 9,119 cases were linked to extreme heat.

Heat is the largest weather-health burden nationally. The next question is whether this risk is evenly distributed across Australia — and who carries the greatest share.

Where the risk is concentrated

Spatial pattern

Hover each SA2 to inspect local risk conditions and compare HHRI scores across regions.

Takeaway: HHRI reflects compound risk — not temperature alone.

How to read this map

Darker SA2s have higher relative HHRI. Grey areas are excluded — they have no resident population or sit outside the mainland analysis scope.

WHAT HHRI CAPTURES

  • Exposure — local heat and environmental pressure
  • Vulnerability — housing, health and social disadvantage
  • Adaptive capacity — support access and local resilience

WHERE TO LOOK

  • Northern Australia — high exposure + vulnerability
  • Remote regions — stronger compound risk
  • Major cities — generally lower but locally varied
Excluded regions
  • Other Territories (4 SA2s): Christmas Island, Cocos (Keeling) Islands, Jervis Bay, Norfolk Island
  • No resident population (~142 SA2s): national parks, industrial zones, airports, ports and military areas
  • Migratory / offshore / shipping zones: no fixed residents

Individual drivers explain local scores. The next question is whether some states are systematically more exposed than others — and how much variation exists within each state.

How much do areas vary within each state?

05 —

The highest-risk communities need targeted local responses

High-risk SA2s are driven by different combinations of pressure — which means one national solution cannot address all of them.

Final synthesis

The highest-risk SA2s are driven by different combinations of pressure

Each row is a high-risk SA2 and each column is a risk component. Darker cells indicate stronger component scores. Click any row to see that SA2’s planning profile below.

How to read this chart

  • Rows = highest-risk SA2s
  • Columns = risk components
  • Darker cells = stronger component score

Look for:

  • rows with several dark cells
  • repeated dark columns (common drivers)
  • different patterns across rows (varied profiles)

Most high-risk SA2s combine exposure with vulnerability, housing, health or adaptive-capacity pressures.

What this means for planning

Heat-health risk is not just a climate issue — it is also a housing, health, and social vulnerability issue. Each high-risk SA2 has a different dominant pressure, which means each needs a different response.

Final insight: The highest-risk SA2s need targeted local responses, not one national solution.

Response types by dominant driver

High exposure

Cooling infrastructure, heat alerts and shade programs

High housing pressure
