01 —

Why heat-health risk matters

National injury data shows why extreme heat is the starting point before mapping where heat-health risk is highest.

Key finding

Heat is the dominant weather-health burden

Extreme heat records 7,104 injury hospitalisations, far above bushfire, cold, rain and storms.

Heat represents 78% of listed extreme-weather injury hospitalisations.

Several years exceed 1,000 cases, showing this is a recurring health burden.

National burden

Heat causes the largest injury burden

Time pattern

Weather injuries return year after year

Proportion

Heat dominates the burden

78% of all recorded extreme-weather injury hospitalisations were heat-related. 7,104 of 9,119 cases were linked to extreme heat.

With heat established as the dominant weather-health burden, the next step is to map where heat-health risk is concentrated.

02

Where the risk is concentrated

Heat-health risk is uneven across Australia. The map reveals where exposure, vulnerability and adaptive capacity combine into higher local risk.

Spatial pattern

Heat-health risk is not evenly spread

Hover each SA2 to inspect local risk conditions and compare overall HHRI scores across regions.

The SA2 map shows the overall Heat-Health Risk Index, where darker areas have higher relative risk.

Takeaway: The map should not be read as temperature alone. It combines heat exposure with social, housing, health and adaptive-capacity factors.

How to read this map

Darker SA2s have higher relative heat-health risk. The score combines heat exposure, social and housing vulnerability, health status and adaptive capacity, so it should not be read as temperature alone.

  • Exposure: local heat and environmental pressure
  • Vulnerability: housing, health and social disadvantage
  • Adaptive capacity: support access and local resilience

Use this as a guide: first scan for high-risk clusters, then hover to identify which component is driving risk in each SA2.

Hotspot ranking

The highest-risk SA2s are concentrated in just three states

A ranked lollipop chart makes the map easier to interrogate.

Each dot is one SA2, coloured by state. The top 10 highest-risk SA2s are all drawn from Queensland, Western Australia, and the Northern Territory — confirming the geographic concentration visible in the map.

Takeaway: All ten highest-risk SA2s are in QLD, WA, or NT, confirming that compound risk is strongly concentrated in northern and remote Australia.

Risk distribution

Queensland and WA have the highest share of top-risk SA2s

100% stacked bars show how SA2s fall across five risk quintiles within each state or territory.

A larger rightmost (darkest) segment indicates a higher proportion of that state's SA2s fall in the highest risk band. Compare across states to identify which regions contain more concentrated risk.

Takeaway: Queensland and Western Australia have a noticeably larger share of SA2s in the highest risk quintile — risk is geographically concentrated, not evenly distributed.

Knowing where risk is highest raises the question of why. The following charts decompose the HHRI into its five components to reveal which specific pressures are driving local scores.

03 —

What drives heat-health risk?

Heat-health risk is highest where multiple pressures overlap. This section separates the index into exposure, vulnerability, housing, health, age and socioeconomic conditions to show which combinations create the strongest local concern.

Risk increases when these overlap

Exposure + Vulnerability + Housing + Health + Age + Disadvantage

Compound pressure

High-risk SA2s are not just hot places

They are places where heat exposure combines with:

  • Social vulnerability — households less equipped to cope during heat events
  • Housing pressure — overcrowding and poor-quality dwellings reduce resilience
  • Poorer health — pre-existing conditions increase heat sensitivity
  • Older populations — greater physiological vulnerability to heat stress
  • Socioeconomic disadvantage — fewer resources to adapt or recover

The upper-right quadrant of the chart marks the clearest compound-risk zone: SA2s where high heat exposure and high social vulnerability occur together.

Main driver — quadrant scatterplot

Exposure becomes more serious when vulnerability is high

Each dot is one SA2. Colour shows HHRI risk score. Shaded zones mark the four quadrant types. Use the dropdown to explore states.

The upper-right quadrant identifies SA2s where high heat exposure coincides with high social vulnerability — the clearest compound-risk pattern. NT and QLD SA2s dominate this zone, but Queensland and Western Australia also contribute concentrated clusters.

Takeaway: The most concerning SA2s combine high exposure with high vulnerability. Neither dimension alone explains the highest HHRI scores.

Dual pressure — density view

Housing and health pressures can compound risk

Each cell groups SA2s with similar scores. Darker = higher mean HHRI. Use the state dropdown to compare.

SA2s with both poorer housing conditions and poorer health status may face greater difficulty coping during extreme heat. The darkest cells in the upper-right confirm where both dimensions align and risk is highest.

Takeaway: Simultaneous housing and health disadvantage reliably predicts higher overall HHRI scores.

Population structure — bubble chart

Older populations add planning priority

Each bubble is one SA2. Size = population. Colour = HHRI risk band. Upper-right = priority zone.

Larger bubbles in the high-exposure, high older-population zone represent communities where both demographic vulnerability and population size may increase planning priority during heat events.

Takeaway: Communities combining high heat exposure with large older populations warrant targeted planning attention regardless of their overall HHRI rank.

Socioeconomic pattern

Disadvantage is associated with higher heat-health risk

Each bar shows mean HHRI for SA2s in that SEIFA decile. Decile 1 = most disadvantaged.

Grouping SA2s by SEIFA decile makes the socioeconomic pattern easier to read than individual points. SA2s in the most disadvantaged deciles consistently record higher average HHRI scores, confirming that socioeconomic conditions shape heat-health risk.

Takeaway: SA2s in the bottom two SEIFA deciles average notably higher HHRI scores — socioeconomic disadvantage amplifies heat-health risk.

Individual drivers explain local scores, but comparing states reveals whether some regions are systematically more exposed than others — and how much variation exists within each state.

04

Comparing places

State summaries reveal broad patterns, while the SA2 map shows local variation.

Component mix

Exposure dominates in the NT and WA; other states show more balanced profiles

Normalised bars show the average relative contribution of each risk component by state.

The 100% normalised bars reveal that the Northern Territory’s risk is dominated by heat exposure, while states like Victoria and Tasmania show a more even distribution across housing, health, and vulnerability components.

Takeaway: The Northern Territory records the highest relative exposure component of any state or territory. New South Wales and Victoria show more balanced mixes — meaning targeted responses will need to differ by region.

Within-state variation

State averages hide large local differences

Box plots show how HHRI scores vary across SA2s within each state. Wider boxes and longer whiskers indicate greater local variation.

Each box shows the middle 50% of SA2 scores. The dark line marks the median and dots show statistical outliers. Queensland is highlighted — it shows one of the widest spreads, meaning its local areas differ strongly from each other.

Takeaway: State-level averages can hide sharp local differences. This is why SA2-level mapping remains essential for identifying where heat-health risk is concentrated.

05

Final focus: the highest-risk areas

The final section returns to the highest-risk SA2s and shows which risk components drive their scores.

Final synthesis

The highest-risk SA2s are driven by different combinations of pressure

Each row is a high-risk SA2 and each column is a risk component. Darker cells indicate stronger component scores.

How to read this chart

  • Rows = highest-risk SA2s
  • Columns = risk components
  • Darker cells = stronger component score

Look for:

  • rows with several dark cells
  • repeated dark columns (common drivers)
  • different patterns across rows (varied profiles)

Most high-risk SA2s combine exposure with vulnerability, housing, health or adaptive-capacity pressures.

What this means

The highest-risk SA2s are not just the hottest places. They are areas where heat exposure combines with vulnerability, housing, health or adaptive-capacity pressures — supporting targeted local responses rather than one national solution.

Final insight: Heat-health risk is not evenly distributed and not driven by heat alone. The highest-risk areas need local responses that address both environmental exposure and social vulnerability.
