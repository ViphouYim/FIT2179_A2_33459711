Extreme heat is not just a weather problem.

Heat-health risk increases where high exposure overlaps with housing pressure, poorer health, older populations and socioeconomic vulnerability.

This dashboard is designed for public health planners, emergency managers, and local councils seeking to identify where targeted heat interventions are most needed.

01 —

Why heat-health risk matters

National injury data shows why extreme heat is the starting point before mapping where heat-health risk is highest.

Key finding

Heat is the dominant weather-health burden

Extreme heat records 7,104 injury hospitalisations, far above bushfire, cold, rain and storms.

Heat represents 78% of listed extreme-weather injury hospitalisations.

Several years exceed 1,000 cases, showing this is a recurring health burden.

National burden

Heat causes the largest injury burden

Time pattern

Weather injuries return year after year

Proportion

Heat dominates the burden

78% of all recorded extreme-weather injury hospitalisations were heat-related. 7,104 of 9,119 cases were linked to extreme heat.

With heat established as the dominant weather-health burden, the next step is to map where heat-health risk is concentrated.

02

Where the risk is concentrated

Heat-health risk is uneven across Australia. The map reveals where exposure, vulnerability and adaptive capacity combine into higher local risk.

Spatial pattern

Heat-health risk is not evenly spread

Hover each SA2 to inspect local risk conditions and compare overall HHRI scores across regions.

The SA2 map shows the overall Heat-Health Risk Index, where darker areas have higher relative risk.

Takeaway: The map should not be read as temperature alone. It combines heat exposure with social, housing, health and adaptive-capacity factors.

How to read this map

Darker SA2s have higher relative heat-health risk. The score combines heat exposure, social and housing vulnerability, health status and adaptive capacity, so it should not be read as temperature alone.

  • Exposure: local heat and environmental pressure
  • Vulnerability: housing, health and social disadvantage
  • Adaptive capacity: support access and local resilience

Use this as a guide: first scan for high-risk clusters, then hover to identify which component is driving risk in each SA2.

Hotspot ranking

The highest-risk SA2s are concentrated in just three states

A ranked lollipop chart makes the map easier to interrogate.

Each dot is one SA2, coloured by state. The top 10 highest-risk SA2s are all drawn from Queensland, Western Australia, and the Northern Territory — confirming the geographic concentration visible in the map.

Takeaway: All ten highest-risk SA2s are in QLD, WA, or NT, confirming that compound risk is strongly concentrated in northern and remote Australia.

Risk distribution

Queensland and WA have the highest share of top-risk SA2s

100% stacked bars show how SA2s fall across five risk quintiles within each state or territory.

A larger rightmost (darkest) segment indicates a higher proportion of that state's SA2s fall in the highest risk band. Compare across states to identify which regions contain more concentrated risk.

Takeaway: Queensland and Western Australia have a noticeably larger share of SA2s in the highest risk quintile — risk is geographically concentrated, not evenly distributed.

Knowing where risk is highest raises the question of why. The following charts decompose the HHRI into its five components to reveal which specific pressures are driving local scores.

03

What drives risk?

Risk is highest where multiple pressures overlap. These charts separate the index into exposure, housing, health and social vulnerability.

Driver overlap

The states most at risk face high exposure and high vulnerability simultaneously

Each row is a state, sorted from highest to lowest mean HHRI. Orange dots mark mean heat exposure; blue dots mark mean social vulnerability. The line between them shows the gap — a short line means both risks are aligned.

NT and QLD sit at the top with the closest pairing of high exposure and high vulnerability, making their SA2s the hardest to protect. States like Victoria and ACT have lower exposure but moderate vulnerability — a different policy challenge.

Takeaway: Northern Territory leads on both dimensions — high heat exposure and high social vulnerability together produce its consistently top-ranked HHRI scores.

Dual pressure — density map

High-risk SA2s cluster toward both high housing and high health pressure

Each cell groups SA2s with similar scores. Darker = higher mean HHRI. Use the state dropdown to compare.

This 2D binned heatmap solves the overplotting problem of 2,400+ dots. Each cell is a bin of SA2s with similar housing and health index values; colour shows their mean HHRI. Cells toward the top-right with the darkest shading identify where both risk dimensions coincide.

Takeaway: The darkest cells — highest mean HHRI — cluster in the top-right of the grid, confirming that simultaneous housing and health disadvantage reliably predicts higher overall risk.

Population structure — jitter strip

Higher-risk quintiles tend to have more older residents

Each dot is one SA2. The red bar is the mean for that quintile. Dots jitter horizontally to reduce overlap.

Each dot is one SA2, coloured by risk quintile and randomly jittered left-right within its band to show the full distribution without overplotting. The bold red tick marks the mean % of residents aged 65+ for each quintile — revealing whether older populations are systematically more prevalent in high-risk areas.

Takeaway: Mean older-population share rises consistently from the lowest to the highest risk quintile, confirming that demographic ageing compounds heat-health risk in predictable ways.

Socioeconomic pattern

Disadvantage amplifies heat-health risk

Each tick is one SA2. The red bar is the mean HHRI per decile.

Each tick mark is one SA2. The red bar shows the mean HHRI within each SEIFA decile. SA2s in lower deciles (more disadvantaged) consistently record higher average risk scores.

Takeaway: SA2s in the bottom two SEIFA deciles average notably higher HHRI scores, confirming that socioeconomic disadvantage systematically compounds heat-health risk.

Individual drivers explain local scores, but comparing states reveals whether some regions are systematically more exposed than others — and how much variation exists within each state.

04

Comparing places

State summaries reveal broad patterns, while the SA2 map shows local variation.

Component mix

Exposure dominates in the NT and WA; other states show more balanced profiles

Normalised bars show the average relative contribution of each risk component by state.

The 100% normalised bars reveal that the Northern Territory’s risk is dominated by heat exposure, while states like Victoria and Tasmania show a more even distribution across housing, health, and vulnerability components.

Takeaway: The Northern Territory records the highest relative exposure component of any state or territory. New South Wales and Victoria show more balanced mixes — meaning targeted responses will need to differ by region.

Within-state variation

Queensland’s SA2s span the widest range of risk scores

Box plots show within-state HHRI distribution. Whiskers extend to the minimum and maximum value.

The box shows the middle 50% of SA2 risk scores in each state. Whiskers extend to the minimum and maximum. Wide boxes or long whiskers indicate states where local risk varies dramatically.

Takeaway: Queensland shows the widest spread from its lowest to highest SA2, confirming that state-level averages mask highly uneven local conditions — which is why SA2-level mapping remains essential.

05

Final focus: the highest-risk areas

The final focus returns to the highest-risk SA2s and shows which risk components drive their scores.

Final synthesis

The highest-risk SA2s are driven by different combinations of pressures

Darker cells indicate higher component scores. Compare rows to see how risk composition varies across the top 10 SA2s.

Each row is one SA2; each column is a risk component. Darker shading indicates a higher score on that component. Reading across a row reveals which pressures combine to make that area high-risk — and why a single national response would be insufficient.

Takeaway: Priority areas are not high-risk for one reason alone. Most combine high exposure with elevated vulnerability, housing, or health scores — requiring place-specific interventions rather than generic national responses.

How to read this chart

Darker cells indicate higher component scores. Compare each row to see how risk composition changes across the top 10 SA2s.

Most high-risk areas do not score high on every component. Instead, each region shows a different mix of exposure, housing, health, vulnerability, and adaptive capacity.

Why this matters: targeted local responses work better than a single nationwide solution.

What this means

Heat-health risk is not simply a map of hot places. The highest-risk SA2s are places where heat exposure combines with social vulnerability, housing condition risk, health status risk and limited adaptive capacity. Mapping these factors together makes the risk easier to see, compare and act on.

Domain, Audience, and Method

Domain: climate-related heat-health risk in Australian SA2 regions.

Who: public health planners, local councils, emergency managers, and policy audiences.

What: a multi-chart dashboard integrating national burden, spatial risk, drivers, and top-risk profiles.

Why: to identify where heat-health risk is highest and which factors should be targeted first.
